It is perfectly normal to feel that "uncomfortable feeling." You’ve essentially bumped into the fact that Japanese is a **predicate-heavy** language, whereas English is a **subject-heavy** language.

The creator you're watching is right: the grouping is based on **how the words behave** (morphology and syntax) rather than what they mean. In Japanese, "cold" isn't just a quality; it's practically a state of being that acts like an action.

Here is why that grouping is the "secret sauce" to understanding Japanese grammar.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 1. Nouns and *Na*-Adjectives: The "Static" Group

In Japanese, **Nouns** and ***Na*-adjectives** are essentially the same species. Linguists often call *na*-adjectives **"Adjectival Nouns."**

### Why they are grouped together:

- **They cannot conjugate:** You cannot change the ending of a noun or a *na*-adj to change the tense.

- **They need a "Bridge":** To say "It is \[X\]," both need the copula (**da** or **desu**).

  - *Noun:* Gakusei **desu** (It is a student).

  - *Na-Adj:* Kirei **desu** (It is pretty).

- **Past Tense:** They both use **deshita** or **datta**.

  - *Noun:* Gakusei **deshita**.

  - *Na-Adj:* Kirei **deshita**.

### The only difference:

How they stick to other nouns.

- Nouns use **no** (*Neko **no** omocha* — The cat's toy).

- *Na*-adjectives use **na** (*Kirei **na** omocha* — A pretty toy).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. Verbs and *I*-Adjectives: The "Self-Powered" Group

This is where English speakers usually trip. In Japanese, ***i*-adjectives** are actually **"Adjectival Verbs."** \### Why they are grouped together:

- **They conjugate themselves:** They don't need a "bridge" (copula) to tell you the tense. The "is-ness" is built into the word.

- **The Past Tense "Tail":** Both change their endings to indicate the past.

  - *Verb:* Taberu Tabe**ta** (Ate).

  - *I-Adj:* Samui Samu**katta** (Was cold).

- **Negative Form:** Both have specific inflections to mean "not."

  - *Verb:* Iku Ik**anai** (Not go).

  - *I-Adj:* Oishii Oishi**kunai** (Not delicious).

> **The "Desu" Trap:** You will see *i*-adjectives followed by *desu* (e.g., *Samui desu*). In this specific case, the *desu* is **purely for politeness**. It has zero grammatical function regarding tense. You could drop it and the sentence "Samui!" is a complete, grammatically perfect sentence meaning "It is cold!"

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## How to Keep This Clear (The Learner’s Cheat Sheet)

To master this, stop thinking about what the word *is* in English and start asking: **"Does this word have a built-in battery?"**

### Step 1: The "Is-ness" Test

- **Verbs and *I*-Adjectives** have a battery. They can stand alone as a full sentence.

  - *Hashiru.* (I run/will run.)

  - *Takai.* (It is expensive.)

- **Nouns and *Na*-Adjectives** are dead batteries. They need a "power source" (*da/desu*).

  - *Gakusei...* (Student... \[Incomplete sentence\])

  - *Gakusei **da**.* (It is a student.)

### Step 2: The Past Tense "Check"

If you want to say something happened in the past:

- If you have to change the **word itself** (e.g., *Oishii* *Oishikatta*), it’s an ***i*-adjective** or a **verb**.

- If you have to change the **helper word** (e.g., *Desu* *Deshita*), it’s a **noun** or a ***na*-adjective**.

### Step 3: Look at the "Tail"

- Almost all **i-adjectives** end in a "hiragana **i**" () that is not part of a kanji's reading.

- **Verbs** always end in a **"u-column"** sound (, etc.).

- If a word describes a quality but doesn't end in , it’s almost certainly a **na-adjective** (like *Suki*, *Kirei*, or *Benri*).

**Pro-tip:** Treat *i*-adjectives as "Stative Verbs." Instead of "Cold," think of *Samui* as the verb "To-be-cold." If you do that, the fact that it conjugates just like a verb will suddenly stop being weird and start being efficient!

## The "Identity Card" Style

Instead of asking "What is this word?", we ask **"How does this word handle the past?"** By showing the Past Casual and Past Polite side-by-side, you force your brain to see whether the **word** changed or the **helper** changed.

### Card Template

**Front:**

> \[Japanese Word\] + \[Simple Context Phrase\] *(e.g., 寒い — 天気が...)*

**Back:**

> **"Action" Translation:** \[To-be-X\] or \[State-of-X\]
>
> **The Past Tense Test:**
>
> - **Casual:** \[Word changes OR Helper appears\]
>
> - **Polite:** \[The "Sticker" vs. "Engine" check\]
>
> **Verdict:** \[I-Adj/Verb\] or \[Na-Adj/Noun\]

To understand this, we first need to strip away the academic jargon and look at the two basic building blocks of any sentence:

- **The Subject:** The "Who" or "What" (e.g., *I*, *the cat*, *the weather*).

- **The Predicate:** The "What about it?"—everything else that tells you the action or state of being (e.g., *...ran away*, *...is delicious*, *...ate a sandwich*).

When we say English is **subject-heavy** and Japanese is **predicate-heavy**, we are talking about what each language considers *mandatory* to make a proper sentence.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 1. English is "Subject-Heavy" (Obsessed with the "Who")

English grammar is fiercely loyal to the subject. A grammatically correct English sentence **must** have a subject, even if that subject is completely meaningless.

Think about the weather. If you look outside and see rain, you say:

> **"It** is raining."

Who or what is "It"? The sky? Nature? God? "It" doesn't actually mean anything here. It’s just a grammatical placeholder because English text breaks down if a verb doesn't have a subject to lean on.

In English, the subject is the anchor. You state the subject first, and the rest of the sentence follows it.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. Japanese is "Predicate-Heavy" (Obsessed with the "What")

Japanese couldn't care less about placeholders. In fact, if the speaker and the listener already know *who* or *what* is being talked about, mentioning the subject is considered repetitive, unnatural, or even rude.

If it's raining, a Japanese speaker will just say:

> **降っている。** (*Futte iru.* / Raining.)

No "It." No placeholder. Just the action.

If someone asks you, "Did you eat the cake?" in English you say, "**I** ate it." In Japanese, you just say:

> **食べた。** (*Tabeta.* / Ate.)

\[Image comparing English and Japanese sentence structure and subject dropping\]

Because the subject is so often deleted, the **predicate** (the verb or adjective at the very end of the sentence) has to do all the heavy lifting. It has to pack in all the context:

- Is it past or present?

- Is it positive or negative?

- Is the speaker being casual or polite?

- Is it a statement or a question?

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Why this matters for your learning:

In English, you look at the **beginning** of the sentence to see who is driving the action.

In Japanese, you have to train your brain to wait for the **very last word** of the sentence, because that is where the real information lives. That last word (the predicate) contains the tense, the mood, and the politeness.

This is exactly why Japanese *i*-adjectives behave like verbs (conjugating into the past tense by themselves). Since Japanese is predicate-heavy, adjectives aren't just descriptions sitting next to a noun; they are powerful enough to be the entire predicate, standing alone as a complete sentence.

When you say *Samui!* ("Cold!"), you have just spoken a perfect, fully formed Japanese sentence. The predicate did all the work, and the subject wasn't even invited to the party.
