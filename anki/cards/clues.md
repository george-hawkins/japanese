Valid vs. Invalid Front-Side Clues for RTK Cards
------------------------------------------------

> Heisig says it's OK to include a minor clue or two on the front side of an RTK
> Anki card that otherwise has just the keyword. Which clues are valid, and which
> ones essentially break the system?

James Heisig is on board with adding minor clues to the front of your Anki cards,
but he's very specific about *why*. English has a lot of synonyms and homophones,
and since RTK relies on a strict 1:1 mapping between an English keyword and a
Japanese kanji, you need a way to know exactly which keyword you're looking at
without guessing.

**The golden rule:** A valid clue clarifies the *meaning or context of the English
keyword*. An invalid clue *leaks information about the structure or components of
the Japanese kanji*.

## 🟩 Valid Clues — the "Nuance Clarifiers"

Valid clues exist solely to clear up English ambiguity. They help your brain lock
onto the correct, unique keyword so you can retrieve the correct story.

**1. Synonym & homophone disambiguation** — when two keywords sound identical or
mean almost the same thing, a parenthetical note is essential.

- `Right (direction)` vs. `Right (correct)`
- `To cut (with scissors)` vs. `To cut (chop/slice)`
- `Companion (vs. friend)`

**2. Part of speech / grammatical hints** — when a keyword can be a noun or a verb,
which can confuse story retrieval.

- `Bear (noun – animal)` vs. `Bear (verb – to endure)`
- `Saw (tool)` vs. `Saw (past tense of see)`

**3. "Not [X]" anti-hints** — if you keep writing the kanji for a *similar* keyword,
explicitly tell your brain what not to look for.

- `Gaze (not "look" or "see")`

## 🟥 Invalid Clues — the "System Breakers"

Invalid clues short-circuit the memory work. They give your brain a mechanical or
visual cheat sheet, which means you aren't actually practicing retrieval of the
Heisig story. If you need these to pass a card, your story is too weak.

**1. Primitive / component leaks** — giving away which radicals are inside the kanji
defeats the purpose of the method.

- ❌ `Bright (sun + moon)`
- ❌ `Forest (three trees)`

You bypass the story entirely. Your brain just glues the pieces together
mechanically, which fails long-term.

**2. Positional / structural hints** — telling yourself where components go or how
the kanji is shaped.

- ❌ `Assist (radical on the left)`
- ❌ `Word (mouth is at the bottom)`

Your imaginative memory should dictate the layout based on the narrative.

**3. Stroke counts** — using the physical complexity of the kanji as a hint.

- ❌ `Night (8 strokes)`

It's a meta-hint. You'll start guessing the kanji based on its size rather than its
meaning.

**4. Phonetic clues (readings)** — including the on'yomi or kun'yomi on the front of
an RTK 1 card.

- ❌ `Water (mizu / SUI)`

RTK Phase 1 is strictly about mapping meaning to form. Introducing readings at this
stage pollutes the visual/imaginative connection with auditory data, which Heisig
explicitly warns slows down initial acquisition.

## Summary Checklist for the Front Side

| Front card element     | Status        | Purpose                                    |
| ---------------------- | ------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| English keyword        | 🟩 Mandatory  | The core prompt.                           |
| Context / synonym note | 🟩 Valid      | Keeps keywords distinct.                   |
| Primitive names        | 🟥 Invalid    | Cheating the recall process.               |
| Stroke count / layout  | 🟥 Invalid    | Crutches that weaken memory.               |
| Japanese readings      | 🟥 Invalid    | Bloats the card; saved for RTK 2 / vocab.  |
