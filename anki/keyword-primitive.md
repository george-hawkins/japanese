# Why 圭 means "squared jewel" as a kanji but "ivy" as a primitive

> **Q:** In RTK, do you know why Heisig gives 圭 the meaning *squared jewel* as a character and *ivy* as a primitive? Why not *ivy* in both cases?

It's a classic "Heisig-ism." To understand why James Heisig splits the personality of 圭, you have to look at his core philosophy: **mnemonics over etymology**. He isn't trying to teach you the historical evolution of the characters; he's trying to give you a mental hook that's easy to visualize and manipulate in a story.

## The kanji meaning: "squared jewel"

Historically 圭 refers to a ritual jade tablet or scepter given to lords in ancient China. It was often rectangular or pointed at the top — hence a "squared jewel." Heisig sticks to a semi-literal definition here because, as a standalone kanji, that is what it *is*.

The problem: if you used "squared jewel" as a building block in every complex kanji that contains it, your stories would become incredibly clunky. Imagine fitting "ritual jade scepter" into a story about a street or a cliff.

## The primitive meaning: "ivy"

When 圭 acts as a primitive, Heisig abandons the jewel entirely and calls it **ivy**.

- **Visual logic:** the character is two "earth" (土) primitives stacked on top of each other. One earth climbing on top of another looks like a vine growing upward.
- **Storytelling utility:** ivy is a fantastic image — it's sticky, it grows on things, it covers surfaces, and it's vivid.

## Why not "ivy" for both?

If he called the kanji itself "ivy," you'd learn the wrong meaning for the character. In a Japanese text, 圭 doesn't mean a plant — it appears in names or refers to that specific jewel shape.

The split strategy serves two purposes:

- **Precision** — you learn the actual meaning of the standalone character (squared jewel).
- **Flexibility** — you use a more active image (ivy) to build 10+ other kanji without the stories turning bizarrely academic.

## The "ivy" primitive in action

Notice how much easier "ivy" is to work with:

| Kanji | Meaning | Heisig story logic |
|-------|---------|--------------------|
| 街 | Street | The boulevard (行) is covered in ivy (圭). |
| 崖 | Cliff | Ivy (圭) hanging down from a cliff. |
| 桂 | Japanese Judas-tree | A tree (木) that looks like it's covered in ivy (圭). |

**The takeaway:** Heisig gives you the *jewel* so you know what the word means, but he gives you the *ivy* so you can actually remember how to write the other characters that contain it. Use the jewel for the destination, but the ivy for the journey.
