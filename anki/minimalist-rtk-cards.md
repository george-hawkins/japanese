# Minimalist Anki Cards for RTK

This page covers building Anki cards from scratch while reading the RTK book, using [Kanji Koohii](https://kanji.koohii.com/) for story inspiration. The idea is to deliberately go minimal, with no readings or vocab layered in until RTK is finished. 風 (Wind) is used as the running example throughout.

## One card per kanji — production only

**Use a single card: Keyword → Kanji** (the "production" card).

Heisig's whole philosophy is isolating *meaning* from *reading* to avoid cognitive overload. Baking in readings now is trying to learn two languages at once. So during RTK, you only train one direction: see the keyword, produce the kanji.

If you can draw the kanji from a keyword, you'll naturally recognize it in the wild later. Adding a reverse card (Kanji → Keyword) roughly doubles your review load — the difference between 15 and 30+ minutes a day. Across 2,000+ characters, that's the mountain that makes people quit.

## The card template

Create a **single Note Type** with these fields, so you can restyle every card at once later:

| Field | Content (例: 風) |
|---|---|
| **Keyword** | Wind |
| **Kanji** | 風 |
| **Primitives** | Hood + Gnat *(or Drop + Insect, depending on edition)* |
| **Story** | A giant wind picks up a hood and traps a gnat inside it. |
| **Frame #** | *(optional — for referencing the book)* |

**Layout:**

- **Front:** just the Keyword.
- **Back:** Kanji (large font), Primitives, and Story.

> **The "pencil and paper" rule:** Always review with a notebook or whiteboard. See "Wind," physically write 風, *then* flip the card. If it matches, you pass.

**On stroke order:** rather than pasting an image per card (tedious), install a stroke-order font like **KanjiStrokeOrders**, or an Anki add-on (e.g. AJATT Tools: Japanese) that generates the diagrams automatically.

## Using Koohii without burning out

Inventing 2,000+ memorable stories yourself is a recipe for burnout — leaning on Koohii's collective (and often weird) imagination is far more efficient.

- **Sort by "Most Popular."** Top-rated stories usually use the most *standard* primitives, which keeps your internal "alphabet" consistent.
- **Embrace the visual shock factor.** A boring story is forgotten; a violent, absurd, or otherwise weird one sticks. Your brain clings to outliers.
- **Don't copy-paste blindly.** Read the top three, pick the one that *clicks* instantly. If none click, combine elements of two.
- **Keep primitive names consistent.** Calling something "weather vane" on one card and "wind" on another creates mental friction.

**Grading rule:** you don't need a dictionary definition on the back — only whether you got Heisig's keyword right. See 風, say "Wind" → pass. Say "Breeze" → technically fail yourself, because "Breeze" might belong to another kanji later (like 颯).

**Leeches:** if you keep missing a card, don't just repeat it — *rewrite the story*. A persistent miss usually means the mnemonic isn't vivid or weird enough.

## Why no reverse (Kanji → Keyword) card?

> *"I don't believe being able to go from the keyword to the kanji implies you can do the reverse. So do you think the reverse process isn't so valuable?"*

It feels like leaving half the job undone, but production-only is a calculated bet on how memory and Japanese actually work:

**1. Recall is stronger than recognition.** Production (blank slate → build every stroke) is the harder cognitive task; recognition (see it, remember a vibe) is the easier one. Master the hard task and the easy one comes nearly free — you rarely meet someone who can *write* 2,000 kanji from memory but can't *recognize* them on a page.

**2. The keyword trap.** The keyword is temporary scaffolding, not the goal. A Kanji → Keyword card glues 風 to the single English word "Wind" — which can actively mislead you later, when 風 shows up in 風邪 (*kaze*, a cold) or 台風 (*taifū*, typhoon). Production cards teach *structure*; recognition cards risk welding you to one gloss.

**3. The efficiency tax.** A second card per kanji means double the daily reviews (20 new → 40 cards), double the time, and a much higher burnout risk. You halve the mountain without halving the progress.

**4. Recognition happens later anyway.** After RTK you move to vocab/sentences (Core 2k and the like), where *every* card is recognition (Kanji → Meaning/Reading). You'll master reading more naturally there, with real words.

> **The safety-net compromise:** Don't build a second card. Instead, during production review, after you write 風, glance at it and ask: *"If I saw this in a book, would I know it's 'Wind'?"* The answer is almost always yes — you just spent the effort constructing it.
