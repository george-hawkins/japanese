# Learning Kanji in Context

A conversation about whether to learn kanji in isolation, how much effort to spend isolating individual readings inside vocabulary, and how to set up Anki cards to reflect that.

## The premise: context over isolation

Memorizing a single character with its full list of five-plus readings, before knowing a single word those readings appear in, is one of the fastest ways to burn out. Learning kanji in context as vocabulary is the right instinct. The remaining question is what to *do* with the readings as they come up.

## The anchor-word strategy

Don't treat readings as a separate task to memorize. Treat your vocabulary words as **anchors** that pin a reading to a character:

- Learn 学校 (gakkou — school), and you've anchored *gaku* to 学 and *kou* to 校.
- Learn 学生 (gakusei — student), and 学 = *gaku* is reinforced.
- When 大学 (daigaku — university) shows up later, your brain clicks: *that gaku piece again*. You never "studied" the reading — you recognized a recurring part.

> Learn the reading required for the word in front of you. Ignore the rest. They'll show up in other vocabulary later, and you'll learn them then.

Take 生 — it has over a dozen readings (*sei, shou, i-kiru, u-mu, nama…*). If you meet it in 生魚 (namazakana — raw fish) and stop to drill all twelve "just in case," you've stopped learning Japanese and started studying linguistics.

## Notice the split — be a conscious observer, not a passive passenger

The key clarification: you *should* actively know where the cut falls between characters. This is the difference between owning a reusable **Lego kit** and memorizing one fused block of sound.

- **The "ga-kusei" error:** if you think 学 is *ga*, then 学校 (gakkou) becomes "ga-kkou" in your head, nothing matches, and every word feels like starting from scratch.
- **The "gaku-sei" win:** knowing the split means your brain says *there's that gaku piece again — I know that one.*

The method is **notice, don't drill**. When a new word appears, spend three seconds mapping sounds to characters:

> Look at 学生. See the furigana — がく over the first, せい over the second. Think: "In this word, this one's *gaku* and that one's *sei*. Noted." Move on.

That three-second mapping is the active help your brain needs. You're not memorizing a dictionary entry; you're confirming which Lego piece you're looking at — a quick handshake with the kanji to acknowledge who's who.

This mirrors how native children learn: they read books where every kanji carries furigana, and after seeing kanji and reading side by side thousands of times, *every time I see 学, the little letters say gaku* becomes automatic. Your job isn't to memorize the split — it's to **verify** it while reading.

### The checklist for a new compound

Encountering 先生 (sensei):

1. **What does the whole word mean?** Teacher / master.
2. **Where's the split?** 先 = *sen*, 生 = *sei*.
3. **Seen these pieces before?** Wait — 生 was in 学生, and it was *sei* there too. Cool.

No lists, no drills. Just laying eyes on how the bricks fit while you build the wall, rather than studying a blueprint in isolation.

## Bonus: phono-semantic components

Worth *noticing* (not drilling): about 80% of higher-level kanji are phono-semantic compounds — one part hints at meaning, another at sound. Characters sharing the 方 component often share its reading:

| Kanji | Reading | Meaning |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 方 | hou | direction |
| 訪 | hou | to visit |
| 放 | hou | to release |
| 防 | hou | to prevent |

Spotting this while learning vocabulary isn't memorizing a reading — it's a cheat code.

## How to structure the Anki cards

A Goldilocks problem: too much help on the card defeats the purpose, too little leaves gaps.

### Front: zero help

No furigana on the front. If you see 日[ni]本[hon]語[go], your eyes skip the kanji and just read the brackets — you're training yourself to read brackets, not recognize kanji.

> **Front:** 日本語を話します。

### Back: the mapped reading

A flat kana string (にほんごをはなします) isn't enough. If you got the sentence right but were secretly unsure whether 語 was *go* or 話 was *hana* vs *hanashi*, a flat string won't expose it. Put the reading **above each kanji** so you can do a split-second visual audit:

> **Back:** 日[に]本[ほん]語[ご]を 話[はな]します。

Check: *Did I know 話 was hana? Yes. 語 was go? Yes. Moving on.*

### Why "I noticed it when I made the card" isn't enough

Doing the mapping at creation time is great for short-term memory, but Anki is for the long haul. Six months later you need the mapped back to catch **knowledge drift** — when you keep getting the *word* right but start misremembering a character's reading (drifting toward *gak-usei* instead of *gaku-sei*). The bracketed mapping corrects that drift instantly.

### Tooling

The **Anki Japanese Support** add-on automates the format: paste the kanji into a field, press a shortcut, and it generates the 日[に]本[ほん]語[ご] mapping for you.

## Summary

- **Front:** pure kanji — forces the brain to work.
- **Back:** mapped reading above each kanji — confirms the Lego pieces are in the right place.
- Actively *notice* the split when you make the card; let the mapped back guard against drift afterward.
- Don't make "learning readings" a chore. Learn the word and you've learned the reading; learn enough words and you'll start to *feel* how a new kanji should sound before you look it up.
